The €1 Share and the €250 Million Pitch

In March 2023, 127,885 shares in SYNCPILOT SE changed hands at €1.00 each — implying a company worth €5.6 million. The buyer was H.G. Perseus GmbH, a vehicle controlled by two individuals later identified as defendants in Germany's largest tax fraud prosecution.

Eight weeks later, Perseus acquired a second tranche of the same stock at €20.56 per share. Same buyer. Same company. Twenty times the price. No disclosed explanation.

By November 2023, SYNCPILOT's CEO was telling investors the company was valued at €250 million — with a capital raise planned at €450 million. Annual revenue: approximately €12 million.

Twelve months after that, Pictet Asset Management's European Direct Lending fund extended debt financing to SYNCPILOT. Hogan Lovells advised, deploying at least ten named professionals across Munich, Frankfurt, Hamburg, and Düsseldorf.

The question is not whether Pictet had the right to lend. It did. The question is what it was shown.

The Arithmetic

The arithmetic does not support the valuation. European mid-market SaaS companies at €12 million in revenue trade at five to ten times revenue under generous assumptions — a defensible range of €60–120 million. The €250 million figure implies a 37× revenue multiple. Neither is consistent with the company's own transaction record, which had produced a market signal of €5.6 million less than two years earlier.

Three Categories of Non-Disclosure

Three categories of information — if withheld from Pictet's credit committee — would each constitute material non-disclosure under German law.

1
The Perseus Anomaly
A 95% insider discount on shares sold to the same buyer, eight weeks apart, is not a rounding error. It is the kind of transaction that demands an explanation in any credit package. If the explanation existed, it should be producible. If it was absent, Pictet assessed a company's creditworthiness without knowing its shares had recently implied a valuation 45 times lower than the number on the pitch deck.

The LVS Loan
€6.15 million was disbursed to SYNCPILOT under a convertible loan agreement in 2021. It was neither repaid nor converted into equity. The contract may be void — it was never notarised as required. This is a contingent liability sitting on the balance sheet of a company now seeking external debt. Any lender evaluating SYNCPILOT's debt capacity without seeing it was working from an incomplete picture.

The Legal Framework

The applicable German framework is not ambiguous. §264a StGB — Kapitalanlagebetrug criminalises material misrepresentation or concealment in materials used to solicit investment. Prospekthaftung creates civil liability where investment decisions rest on an inaccurate picture of a company's financial condition.

Whether those thresholds are crossed here depends on what Pictet's credit file actually contains: the valuation methodology, the treatment of the Perseus share history, the disclosure or non-disclosure of the LVS liability.
