Eight letters in one morning.

On 13 May 2026, the 11th Civil Chamber of the Augsburg Regional Court ordered Franz-Xaver Bleicher to repay the loan — by then, with interest, €6,706,610.38. The judgment was not the end of the matter. It was the starting gun. The same day, the plaintiff sent eight provisional payment-prohibition orders — vorläufige Zahlungsverbote under §§ 845 and 930 of the German Code of Civil Procedure — reaching from Bleicher's bank to the tax office to six companies in the SyncPilot sphere. In a single coordinated move, the creditor put a hold on every place his money was likely to be.

The Net

A Zahlungsverbot is an instruction to a third party who holds the debtor's money or owes him something: do not pay it out to him. Serve enough of them, in the right places, on the same day, and a debtor can wake up to find his bank, his tax refunds, and his shareholdings all frozen before he has had a chance to move anything. That is what the records show happened here. The eight orders, all dated 13 May 2026, reach across the following targets.

Reaching for the Vault

One line in the Hafner order is quietly the most aggressive of all. The freeze does not stop at accounts and securities. It extends to the contents of his safe-deposit boxes — the physical vault. Creditors reach for safe-deposit boxes when they believe ordinary accounts may not hold the answer. Whether the boxes contain anything of value, the records do not say. But the instruction to seize whatever is inside them is itself a measure of how far the creditor was prepared to go on day one.

The 15% Land Charge

€1,750,000
Land charge over a Westheim property · 15% per annum

Among the assets the Hafner order reaches is a registered land charge (Grundschuld) of €1,750,000 over a property in Westheim, bearing interest at 15% per annum. A rate of 15% is far above an ordinary homeowner's mortgage — it is the kind of return a lender demands in distress, or that a borrower accepts when conventional credit is closed to him. What the records establish: the land charge exists, at that rate, and the creditor moved to attach it. What they do not establish is its purpose. Whether it secures a genuine third-party loan or functions to keep equity out of creditors' reach is not something the documents answer. The pattern — a third property nexus, an unusual rate, attached on the day of judgment — is what is notable, not any single reading of it.

What an Asset Freeze Proves — and What It Does Not

The enforcement orders are powerful, and they are also provisional. They are Arrestpfändungen: one-sided, precautionary attachments, drafted and served by the plaintiff's law firm, that secure the creditor's priority before a debtor can react. They are not a finding by a bailiff that money was actually seized, and they are not evidence that the accounts behind them were empty or that assets had been hidden. It is worth being precise about the line.

What the records show
  • The court ordered repayment of €6,706,610.38 with continuing interest.
  • On the same day, the creditor served eight payment-prohibition orders across the debtor's bank, the tax office, and six SyncPilot-sphere entities.
  • The orders reach accounts, securities, the contents of safe-deposit boxes, share interests, distributions, and a €1.75M land charge.
  • The reach and the timing demonstrate the creditor's intent and the breadth of its claim.
What remains unproven
  • Whether any account was actually emptied or any asset realised.
  • Whether the frozen accounts held meaningful balances.
  • The purpose of the 15% Westheim land charge.
  • Whether Bleicher has posted the 110% security to avert enforcement, or appealed.

What a provisional attachment is

Under §§ 845 and 930 of the German Code of Civil Procedure, a creditor armed with a provisionally enforceable judgment can serve a vorläufiges Zahlungsverbot on third parties who hold the debtor's assets. It freezes those assets and locks in the creditor's rank — its place in the queue — ahead of other claimants. It is a precautionary step, not a completed seizure. The debtor can still avert enforcement by posting security of 110% of the enforceable amount (here, roughly €7.38 million), and the judgment itself remains a Vorbehaltsurteil, subject to the reserved proceedings.
