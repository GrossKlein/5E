The Deal

On 21 May 2021, LVS entered a Cooperation and Call Option Agreement with SyncPilot SE. The terms: €18,750,000 to be paid in tranches, in exchange for 750,000 shares in the holding structure.

Martin Hunstein of GHR Goerg Hunstein Rechtsanwälte drafted the agreement. He served as legal advisor to the transaction. Hunstein — together with his partner Diethard Goerg — owns H.G. Perseus GmbH StBG, the entity that would acquire SyncPilot shares at a 95% discount twenty-two months later.

The contract required notarization to be legally binding under German law. Hunstein was responsible for arranging it.

The Notarization Trap

The contract Hunstein drafted required notarization to be legally binding. Here is what happened instead.

The lawyer who drafted the contract was responsible for notarization. He never completed it. His client paid €6.15 million on the basis of that contract. He now claims the contract is void.

The conflict: the lawyer who collected €6.15 million through a contract he drafted is the same person whose company acquired shares at one-twentieth of market price — in the same company, twenty-two months later.

The €6.15 Million Question: Where Did the Money Go?

LVS transferred €6,150,000 to SyncPilot between April and August 2021. In return, LVS received zero shares, zero equity, and a contract that was never notarized.

SyncPilot's Managing Director — Franz-Xaver Bleicher, the company's founder and largest beneficial owner — controlled where investor funds were directed. The evidence suggests at least two personal uses of company capital during the relevant period:

1. Property acquisition. Bleicher purchased residential property during the period in which LVS funds were flowing into SyncPilot. The timing and source of funds for this acquisition are part of the ongoing investigation. Evidence will be presented in the Evidence Room.

2. Partner buyout. Bleicher acquired the stake of a co-owner in SyncPilot's holding structure. This consolidation of control required capital. The source of that capital — and whether it included investor funds designated for operational purposes — is a central question of this dossier.

Under German law, a managing director's diversion of corporate funds to personal use constitutes Untreue (breach of fiduciary duty, §266 StGB) — a criminal offence carrying a sentence of up to five years. If investor funds were represented as being for corporate purposes but were instead used for personal acquisitions, this additionally constitutes Betrug (fraud, §263 StGB).
