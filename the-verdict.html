Augsburg Regional Court · Case No. 112 O 2848/25 · 13 May 2026

A German court has ordered Franz-Xaver Bleicher to repay €6.7 million — and the creditor is already moving on his assets.

The 11th Civil Chamber of the Augsburg Regional Court, in a Vorbehaltsurteil rendered on 13 May 2026, has found that the loan embedded in the 2021 Cooperation and Call Option Agreement was validly concluded, fully disbursed, and due for repayment since 31 December 2024. Bleicher's attempt on 7 April 2026 to invoke a contractual right of tender — offering Quintus Beteiligungs GmbH shares as set-off — was ruled invalid on two independent grounds. With interest, the sum due reached €6,706,610.38 by the day of judgment. The same day, the plaintiff issued eight asset-freeze orders across Bleicher's entire financial sphere.

Operative Clauses
  1. The defendant is ordered to pay the plaintiff the sum of €6,150,000.00, plus interest thereon at a rate of five percentage points above the applicable base rate since 1 January 2025.
  2. The defendant shall bear the costs of the litigation.
  3. The judgment is provisionally enforceable. The defendant may avert enforcement by providing security in the amount of 110% of the amount enforceable under the judgment.

The Court's Loan Characterisation

The defendant argued that the 2021 agreement contained no genuine loan — that the €18.75 million obligation was, in his words, a "purely technical financing vehicle" rather than the economic core of the transaction. The court rejected this argument. It found that the Cooperation and Call Option Agreement constitutes a composite contract — several independent but economically interrelated contracts forming a single unit — and that the loan embedded within it was validly concluded under §488(1) sentence 2 BGB.

The €6.15 million paid by LVS Hotel & Resorts S.A. across four tranches between April and August 2021 was found to have been disbursed against the loan, not against any option premium. The loan became due for repayment on 31 December 2024, the agreed contractual maturity date.

The Put Option Ruled Forfeited

Section 2.5 of the 2021 agreement gave the defendant a contractual right of tender: if the loan was not repaid by 31 December 2024 and LVS had not exercised its call options, the defendant could compel LVS to acquire Quintus Beteiligungs GmbH shares at a fixed price of €18.80 million, with the tender price set off against the loan. Bleicher exercised this right by letter dated 7 April 2026.

The court found the exercise invalid on two independent grounds.

First, it was untimely. Read together with Section 2.4, the agreement establishes specific points in time at which the right of tender may be exercised — not an open-ended window. The court observed that rights of tender of this type are conventionally exercised within periods of weeks, not months, because shareholders require legal certainty regarding ownership structure. Fifteen months elapsed between the loan's maturity on 31 December 2024 and the formal exercise on 7 April 2026.

Second, the right had been forfeited under §242 BGB (Verwirkung). Forfeiture requires a temporal element and a circumstantial element. Both were present. On the temporal element, the court noted that the right was first raised by the defendant in a letter of 6 October 2025 — itself nine months after the right became enforceable. On the circumstantial element, the court pointed to two distinct conducts of the defendant that entitled LVS to assume the right would not be exercised: the ownership structure of SyncPilot had materially changed since 2021, such that acquisition of the Quintus shares would no longer deliver the contractually intended stake; and the articles of association of Quintus Beteiligungs GmbH had been amended in a manner prejudicial to LVS.

The Ownership Collapse

The 2021 agreement was structured so that LVS would acquire a 15% stake in SyncPilot indirectly, through 10,923 shares (43.69%) in Quintus Beteiligungs GmbH. At the time of contract, Section 1.8 recorded that BOSYNC Beteiligungs UG and Quintus Beteiligungs GmbH together held 41% of SyncPilot.

The court found that this ownership structure has since collapsed. As of the extraordinary general meeting of 5 August 2025, BOSYNC held only 24.1% of SyncPilot. Quintus held nothing at all.

8.8%
Maximum achievable stake
LVS submitted to the court — and the court recorded — that even on payment of the full €18.8 million tender price, the contractually targeted 15% stake in SyncPilot is no longer achievable. At most, an indirect stake of 8.8% would be possible. The bargained-for participation has been diluted to approximately 59% of its original size. See also: The Pictet Pitch.

The Articles Amendment

On 12 January 2026 — three months before Bleicher exercised the right of tender — the articles of association of Quintus Beteiligungs GmbH were amended. Two provisions were newly introduced. A general redemption clause was added at §10(3)(a), permitting shareholders to resolve to redeem the shares of a shareholder who violates the articles or the duty of loyalty in such a manner that, in the case of a partnership, exclusion could be demanded under §134 HGB. A new §11(3) provides that compensation in such cases is set at only 60% of fair market value, or alternatively the lowest amount deemed appropriate in the individual case.

The court characterised these amendments as constituting "an unreasonable hardship for the plaintiff." The late exercise of the right of tender would, in the court's words, force LVS to hold a stake in a company whose articles of association have been amended to its detriment since the agreement on the right of tender was made.

The Defence — and Why It Failed

Bleicher's case is set out in full in the defence brief filed by his counsel, RA Tomanke, on 7 April 2026 — the same letter by which he purported to exercise the right of tender. Read fairly, it rests on four arguments. First, that the documentary procedure (Urkundenprozess) was inadmissible because the dispute turned on contested questions a documents-only process cannot resolve. Second, that his exercise of the right of tender instantly extinguished the loan by set-off, leaving nothing to repay. Third, set-off and accounting in the alternative. Fourth, that LVS's claim was an abuse of right under §242 BGB.

In his own words, the repayment effect "takes effect immediately and requires no cooperation from the plaintiff." The court held the opposite: the same letter that asserted instant extinguishment also proved he had acted fifteen months after the debt fell due, into a company whose ownership had already been hollowed out — the very lateness that triggered forfeiture.

Vorvertrag,
not a sale
The hinge of the case
The argument that won, in the plaintiff's words: the put / right of tender was only a preliminary agreement (Vorvertrag) — an obligation to conclude a future share sale — not a conditional sale that completes on its own. So no purchase price ever fell due, and there was nothing to set off against the loan. LVS cited Gehrlein (WM 2020/1) and BGH authority treating a purchase option as a Vorvertrag, reinforced by the absence of the §15 GmbHG notarisation that an actual share transfer would have required.
